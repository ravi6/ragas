My Bathroom Renovation Saga

Why Bother

As you can see our old bathroom was in very good nick despite its age (44 years). I must admit I like the color scheme, the bidet and nicely moulded bath cum shower enclosure. All my kids enjoyed the bath tub part when they were toddlers. And it was so easy to keep it clean.

With all our friends having progressed to owning large and new homes, there was pressure from my wife Padma to upgrade every thing if we were not moving ever :( . I staved off this pressure for a while. But with my autistic son Kiran growing up to be a man and my mum moving in with us after dad passed away, I had to give in for the reasons of improved accessibility.

Thwarted Attempts

Knowing how I turn everything into a major project, Padma was adamant that the job be outsourced. With my finger in the pie, her attempt to engage disability services as well as an independent contractor to do the job was unsuccessful. The disability services proposals were restrictive due to regulations. They were proposing to change things that were not needed and refusing to adapt to our needs. The private contractor's (unlicensed) quote was pricey (~$14k excluding fixtures and material) When I showed him my spread sheet estimating labor costs that are half as much, he wasn't too impressed.

Finally Padma give up and reluctantly agreed for me take up the job. By now, we spent a lot of time exploring designs and fixtures but haven't consolidated them.

No more Vanity
Removing floor tiles proved very challenging, especially without appropriate power tool. In the past, builders used to cement tiles over a wire mesh that is nailed to the subfloor with a waterproof membrane underneath it. It is a solid construct and the tiles have not moved all these years. Removing the tiles and the cement layer is indeed tough. Instead, lifting the cemment layer along with the mesh is a better option. But it requires some brute strenth, while making sure the subfloor is not damaged. Used a crow bar to hammer my way through. It took few days to complete the job. Not a job for faint hearted and it would be very noisy.

Old Plumbing
It is time to prep the sub floor to lay the tile tray for my shower floor. I purchased this tile tray because it comes with prebuilt slope, is durable and water tight. But it requires to be set on joists so that the shower tray lip will be flush with the surrounding floor tiles. The process involves
  • Cutting the sub floor
  • Adding additional joists to support the tray
  • Setting the tray in with glue ensureing that it is dead level
Sub-floor Removal
Before cutting the subfloor with the circular saw, made sure that no wiring or piping runs were in the way. Despite all precautions, I did hit one nail in the tongue and grove flooring. But luckily I stopped short before damaging the blade. Perhaps, I should have used a magnet to locate the nails before the cut.
Adding Joists
Adding the joists proved very tricky. Since they have to go under the sub floor, there was not much room to rotate the wood into place. I had to shave off ends to rotate and anchorr them over the bearers. Fixing the joists required nailing because they are tough and hold better under twisting loads. Also, I found that it is very hard to drive screws through old hardwood. Application of wax and predrilling made the nailing job lot easier. Nailing in tight spaces was still a problem for me. Ended up using screws in those instances.

I had to make sure the joists are level for the tray to sit on. Any slope will compromise shower water drainage This however proved a daunting task for me even with the assitance of laser leveler. I almost lost one week mucking around. Despite all efforts the leveling was not so good in acros the drain location. It was out by 0.5 degree.

Shower Tile Tray
The shower tile tray is in place. Ideally the tray should have been checked into the studs by 10mm so that after laying cement sheet, the tiled wall bootom edges end up inside the tray. But I did not do it based on the advice from tray supplier. This required me at later stage to use water barrier tape to stop water getting behind the tray when it flows over the tiled wall. You will see the sealing tape in one of the pictures that follow.
Plumbing
Before gluing the tray, had to complete the plumbing fixtures as I would lose ease of access to the piping under the hose. Deciding between the compression fittings or new generation snap in PVC fittings for plumbing was difficult with conflicting opinions. Decided to go with proven copper piping and compression fittings that required no soldering or special tools. Using a pipe bender made the task very neat and satisfying. I was proud of my work despite the fact I took few days to do it.

Installing mixer valve body needed special attention because it should be installed before cement sheet and tiling is done. This requires mounting the mixer with the right offset from the fully tiled wall to ensure the mixer tap fits in properly after tiling. This kept me awake until the mixer tap is fully installed, because an incorrect offset in valve seat will for certain require ripping and re-installation of entire tiled wall. :((

Shower walls prep
I chose to lay water resistant cement sheets all around the bathroom up to two meters height. Had to use almost 300 screws. I must say my drill has never worked harder :)). The but joints of cement sheets are sealed off with special cement. The most brain racking task was working out the optimal way to cut the sheets while they fit neatly across the studs so that they can be screwed on. Normally tradies use large sheets making the job lot easer, but it requies some helping hands as they are heavy.

When laying the sheets one has to make sure the walls are smooth with no ondulations (no more than 1 to 2mm). For an old house like mine normally it would require some shimming or shaving of studs to achieve it. Despite the age of the house, it appeared from my measurements the walls were quite square and smooth with 2 to 3mm variations from smooth plane and I thought I can get away with no major adjustments. But come tiling, I could see how important it is it make the walls very flat, unless we are professional tilers. In few spots I could not aovid having lippage (~1mm) despite the use of leveling clips.

Notice the light blue strip all along the bottom of the shower and along the corner where the shower walls meet. This is the special bituman tape I mentioned earlier that will act as water seal at the bottom seam where shower tray lip meets the cement sheet.

Water Proofing
The standard requires that all wet areas are covered with water proofing membrane (preferably by an expert !!). However, I found that it was a breeze to apply the waterproofing layer. Anyone can with a brush can do it. Just have to make sure all joints and gaps are covered well. I applied three quotes since I had lots of the stuff. After the treatment the bathroom looked so good. The blue color was very calming effect. It will ofcourse be not visible once you tile over it.
Floor Tiling
This was really a mental exercise. One need to plan ahead how you would lay the tiles especially when have to tile around the tray, and the room is not that square. Being me, I used rendering tools to actually lay the tiles in software and then see how it looks before deciding to lay them. The simulation engine I used is called "povray" One can take image of the tile to be layed and see how the bathroom looks before deciding on the tile size and designs.

Apart from painstaking arrangement of tiles to achieve a good pattern, handling and cutting heavy porcealin tiles proved very labour intensive. Thanks to Padma who helped with mixing the glue, keeping time and scheduling the tasks I managed to finish tiling in three weeks. It is fairly excessive time by any standards, but this mainly due to our choice of portable tile cutter that required It is too long a time, cutting. This is because we chose a very portable tile cutter that is relatively easy and allowed us to do tiling at our own pace without incuring rental costs. It became clear a suction handle is needed to position the tile while laying it. The strong surface tension due to large surface makes it hard to maneuver it with bare hands without proper grip. Luckily my mum's shower handle came to the rescue.

The tile cutter I chose was easy to use (made by Makita), battery operated and very portable. But it takes long time to make long and deep cuts that require several passes. But being a wet cutter there is no issue with dust generation. The tool comes with two battery packs that are comact and they last long enough and charge fast. The porcelain tile does use up the diamonod discs quickly. I had to buy additional discs to complete the job.

Lessons Learnt

  • Avoid using large format tiles that are hard to handle, cut and align
  • Stay away from walk-in showers. Too much work and cost to implement. Hard to maintain grout and tiles.
  • Mix your own tile adhesive instead of using expensive pre-mix. It is possible to hand mix the glue in small batches. It sets slightly faster than pre-mix.
  • You can lose weight renovating but it costs :(
  • To learn you must do it :)
