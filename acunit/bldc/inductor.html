Repurposing Brushless DC (BLDC) motor in Air Conditioning unit

The Beginings

This project started with me stripping apart a failed BLDC (Brushless DC motor) from my home air conditioner unit with a desire to repurpose it for other use. The r internal PCB inside the comapct motor was fried due to failed rectifier bridge output capacitort(450V, 120uF) on the external PCB. This bridge circuit on the external PCB provides 350VDC supply to the motor. Also, it provides 15V DC and other control signals to drive the motor.
I have to track back to earlier events that led me to this point. It all started with my home indoor AC unit (Split System) suddenly stopping with a faint spluttering sound in the middle of winter morning. It happened, few days after I have cleaned up the air filter grill on the unit and placed a fragant cotton swab for a warm and sweet ambiance. Now I get the blame for for cleaning up and was held responsible for its failure. So begins my journey into split AC units, BLDC motors and their inner workings. Interested ... then read on.

First Dive

When I tried to start the unit with the remote the green LED on the unit did not respond. The AC unit on the wall controls the blower fan and two small motors that move the grill vanes that direct flow. The unit appeared to have lost power.
Examining the control board, I noted that the 1.5Amp slow blowing ceramaic fuse in the control board was blown. This fuse was soldered to the board. Now, I know why it was not easily repalceable fuse. When it blows, it means there is something seriously wrong in either the PCB board or in the motors that it controls. Trust me one can't just replace the fuse and hope for the best. I blew 200$ in a flash, when I replaced a fried control board and the fuse without investigating when trying to fix my Bosch washing machine. Post event I located a short at motor supply terminals(with a Megger) at 240V.

The Culprit

Further examination of the PCB showed the large output filter capacitor on the bridge rectifier had a slight bulge. I tried to measure its capacitance with multi-meter while in circuit, but it did not give any meaningful results.
And knowing that its failure has implications to BLDC motor supply, repalced it with a new one. With the new capacitor and fuse the PCB was alive and I was able to operate the small louvers that deflect the air (they use tiny motors) with the remote control. But the main blower won't run with unit displaying error code suggesting that there is still a fault in the motor or PCB. Disconnected blower motor and checked the supply voltage pins on the PCB (350V DC voltage for BLDC motor, and 15V for its inernal PCB) they all looked good. So it appears that the problem is likey to be in the motor. Perhaps, the fault in the filter capacitor (failed open), would have introduced too much ripple to BLDC supply voltage and would have damaged it..

A Good Ending

With winter in full swing the pressure was on to get the unit going. So ordered replacement motor. But now the issue is to know the way to remove the old motor from the unit and put the new one in. Fortunately Daikin service manual is up for grabs on the internet. The task of removing the fan motor appeared very daunting with the requirement to disconnect the AC coils from the unit. A big job indeed. Thanks to excellent tips from a nice video by a Daikin service chap, I could get to the blower fan without havning to dismantle the heat exchanger (as per the Daikin maintenance manual) Replacing the motor truned out to be a breeze. With great trepidation I turned on the unit with the remote. It started purring like a cat. The air warmed up with nods of approval from my missus.

Post Mortem

This was the state of PCB inside the motor once I removed the top cover of the motor assembly. Pretty bad ha :((
The stator is an enclosed unit with terminals of windings exposed. The rotor is made of strong permanent magnet (possibly RareEarth Neodemium). The rotor chases the magnetic field that is rotated electronically switching the coils of the stator poles at right times based on the feedback of HAL (magnetic field sensors) senosors embedded in the stator body. Alternately, one could use the back emf signal as a sensing tool. I am still not sure as to which mechanism this motor uses.
Checking the four terminals on the stator block, it appears that there are three coils connected in star fashion. The three coil resistances are 40ohms each. This suggests that the stator windings are unlikely to be damaged and the motor failed due to PCB components that could not tolerate large current spikes because of failed ripple filter capacitor on the high voltage supply to the BLDC motor.
Once the PCB terminals are desoldered off the stator block face, the PCB could be pried out of the stator casing. Then the rotor simply can be pulled out along with the rear bearing.

Resurrecting the Victim

Now that I had some win with the Air Conditioner, and had a whiff of what a BLDC motor is I was wanting to explore if I can use most of the usable part of the motor without the internal and external control boards. The idea was to use Raspberry PI like IOT devices, to provide the necessary control signals and drive the motor with an external motor drive circuit of my own making. It means I have to think of providing control signals while sensing rotor position with either HAL sensors or use sensorless method (using back emf signals from the coils). Playing with MOSFETs to provide switching would be fun for I have never done such things before.
To start off I wanted to work at lower voltage (12-24VDC)level rather than at high DC voltage the motor is designed for. I realise that I have to sacrifice on the motor torque output, becuase of current rating limitation of the stator windings designed for low currents and high voltages. For starters I wanted to get a measure of inductance of the motor windings, so that I can judge the switching frequencies I need to operate at.

Inductance Measurement

Now this has turned into another side alley I had to explore to achieve my goal. After viewing several youtube videos, decided to exploit my oscilloscop's square wave generator (reference) with an LC circuit consisting of known capcitance values. Measuring the ringing frequency of the circuit on square wave edge transitions, the inductance can be estimated from simple theoretical expression for resonant frequency. I learnt, while this looks straight forward, there are many other intricacies to consider if one is really interested in accurate inductance measurement. (eg. dependency of series resistance on the frequency level the inductor is operating at).
Once the resonant frequency at different capcitance values were obtained the inductance of the coil can be estimated from linear regression of Capacitance versus log(Frequency).(Feq = 1/ 2.Pi.sqrt(LC)). The inductor I used was something I cobbled up using a Ferrite core and some 28guage wire wound over it. The ferrite core was from TV power chord noise suppresor. Although, it is easy to get physical construct details such as number of turns, length and cross sectional area of the inductor coil, the magnetic permeability is not all that well defined for ferrite (it has broad range varying by one order of magnitude) Anyway, I had a stab at estimating it and taking the lower end ferrite permiability for the coil. It turned out to be of the order of 14mH.
